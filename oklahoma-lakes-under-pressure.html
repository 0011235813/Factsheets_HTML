Section 2

What is eutrophication?

Eutrophication is when too many nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus get into a lake, causing massive algae growth that harms the water.

🧪

Definition

Eutrophication means nutrient enrichment. As the nutrient load grows, algae respond fast, water gets murkier, and the lake becomes more stressed.

🚜

Where nutrients come from

  • Farms and cropland, especially fertilizers and manure.
  • Lawns, streets, and runoff from developed areas.
  • Sewage and septic systems carrying human waste nutrients.
  • Air pollution that brings nitrogen down from the atmosphere.
📉

What it looks like

The more nutrients a lake receives, the more conditions can shift from clear water to murky water, heavy algae growth, and eventually dangerous bloom conditions.

Section 3

Harmful algal blooms

When lakes get too many nutrients, some blue-green algae can explode in number and produce toxins dangerous to people, pets, fish, and wildlife.

🤒 Human health

RisksSkin rashes, irritation, stomach cramps, nausea, and liver damage at high doses.
Key ruleNever drink bloom water, and keep dogs out too.

🐟 Wildlife and nature

Ecosystem effectsFish kills, loss of oxygen, aquatic plant die-off, bird and wildlife illness, and food-chain disruption.
Common bloom formersMicrocystis, Dolichospermum, Aphanizomenon, and Cylindrospermopsis.

💸 Communities and economy

Community impactsLakes can close to swimming, fishing tourism can drop, home values can fall, and water bills can rise.
Public communicationClear public-facing tools help people understand conditions before they travel or recreate.

Section 4

How OWRB watches lakes

The monitoring story works well as three clear modules: measuring water quality, supporting HABs monitoring partnerships, and sharing the data with people.

Measuring water quality

  • Chlorophyll-a levels
  • Phosphorus and nitrogen
  • Dissolved oxygen
  • Water clarity through Secchi depth
  • Fixed and probabilistic sites

HABs monitoring partnership

  • Led statewide by ODEQ.
  • OWRB and OCC are partners.
  • OWRB lake data informs advisories.
  • OK Travel issues public advisories.

Sharing the data

  • AWQMS for discrete data.
  • Kister's for real-time information.
  • Public data portal and downloadable datasets.
  • Interactive map tools and future dashboards.
Section 5

Why is it getting worse?

Climate change and land use can make eutrophication more likely and more severe over time.

🌡️ Warmer waters

Hotter water favors cyanobacteria and can give harmful blooms a stronger advantage each year.

🌧️ Drought and flood cycles

Drought can concentrate nutrients, then heavy rain can deliver a sudden pulse of runoff into lakes.

🌾 Land use change

More farmland and paved surfaces can move more nutrients toward lakes, especially when natural vegetation is removed.

⏳ Aging reservoirs

Older reservoirs can carry decades of nutrient buildup in lake sediments, making reversal harder over time.

Section 6

What you can do about HABs

This action area is designed to work well on desktop and mobile and can later be customized for different audiences.

1

👀 Spot a bloom

Look for bright green or blue-green water, foamy scums, musty smells, or dead fish nearby.

2

🚫 Stay safe

Do not swim in bloom water, keep dogs and kids on shore, and avoid high-contact recreation in the affected area.

3

🌱 Be part of the fix

Use fertilizer wisely, plant buffers near water, pick up pet waste, and reduce runoff from lawns.

4

📱 Stay informed

Check advisories before lake visits, report suspected blooms, and share information with others.
