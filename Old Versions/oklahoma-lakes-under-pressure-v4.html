Section 2

What is eutrophication?

When too many nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus enter a lake, algae explodes — and the water suffers. Click each card to learn more.

🧪

The definition

Eutrophication means nutrient enrichment. As the nutrient load grows, algae responds fast — water gets murkier, oxygen drops, and the lake becomes more stressed.

Trophic states: Oligotrophic (clean) → Mesotrophic → Eutrophic → Hypereutrophic (dangerous). Most monitored Oklahoma lakes fall somewhere in the middle and are trending in the wrong direction.

Tap to expand ↓
🚜

Where nutrients come from

  • Farms and cropland — fertilizers and manure
  • Lawns, streets, and urban runoff
  • Sewage and septic systems
  • Atmospheric nitrogen deposition

Oklahoma's heavily agricultural landscape means nutrient loading is a documented statewide concern. OWRB's long-term monitoring data tracks nutrient trends across 136 lakes.

Tap to expand ↓
📉

What it looks like

Clear water shifts to murky water, green or brown coloration, surface scums, low oxygen — and eventually conditions that harm fish, wildlife, and people.

Key indicators measured by OWRB: chlorophyll-a, total phosphorus, total nitrogen, Secchi depth, and dissolved oxygen. All five together tell the eutrophication story for each lake.

Section 3

Harmful algal blooms

When nutrients get too high, blue-green algae can produce toxins dangerous to people, pets, fish, and wildlife.

🤒 Human health

Risks

Skin rashes, irritation, stomach cramps, nausea, and liver damage at high doses.

Key rule

Never drink bloom water. Keep pets and children out of visibly affected water.

🐟 Wildlife and nature

Ecosystem effects

Fish kills, oxygen depletion, aquatic plant die-off, and food-chain disruption.

Common bloom formers in Oklahoma

Microcystis, Dolichospermum, Aphanizomenon, Cylindrospermopsis.

💸 Communities and economy

Community impacts

Lakes can close for swimming, fishing tourism can drop, home values can fall, and water treatment costs can rise.

Advisory source

Check OK Travel lake conditions before visiting any Oklahoma lake.

Section 4

How OWRB watches Oklahoma's lakes

Three pillars: measuring water quality, supporting the state HABs partnership, and sharing the data publicly.

📊 Measuring water quality

  • Chlorophyll-a levels
  • Total phosphorus and nitrogen
  • Dissolved oxygen profiles
  • Water clarity (Secchi depth)
  • Fixed and probabilistic sites

🤝 HABs monitoring partnership

Lead agency

ODEQ leads statewide HABs monitoring.

Partners

OWRB and OCC contribute lake data and coordination. OWRB lake data informs public advisories.

🔓 Sharing the data

  • AWQMS for discrete water quality data
  • Kister's for real-time information
  • Public data portal and downloads
  • Interactive maps and future dashboards
Section 5

Why is it getting worse?

Climate change and land use patterns are making eutrophication and HABs more frequent and more severe.

🌡️ Warmer waters

Hotter summers give cyanobacteria a competitive edge — longer bloom seasons and earlier onset each year.

Warmer water also reduces dissolved oxygen, which can stress fish and other aquatic life even without a visible bloom.

Tap to expand ↓

🌧️ Drought and flood cycles

Drought concentrates nutrients in shrinking lakes. Then heavy rain delivers a sudden pulse of runoff — a double hit.

Oklahoma's increasing climate variability means these cycles are becoming more extreme, amplifying bloom risk in vulnerable lakes.

Tap to expand ↓

🌾 Land use change

More farmland and paved surfaces move more nutrients toward lakes, especially when natural vegetation buffers are removed.

Native vegetation along shorelines can filter significant amounts of nutrient runoff before it reaches the water. Loss of these buffers has measurable effects.

Tap to expand ↓

⏳ Aging reservoirs

Most Oklahoma reservoirs were built in the mid-1900s. Decades of nutrient buildup in sediments makes reversal harder over time.

Internal nutrient loading — nutrients released from lake bottom sediments — can sustain blooms even after surface inputs are reduced, making remediation a long-term effort.

Section 6

What you can do

Every person who visits, lives near, or uses Oklahoma's lakes can help.

1

👀 Spot a bloom

Look for bright green or blue-green water, foamy surface scums, musty smells, or dead fish nearby.

2

🚫 Stay safe

Do not swim in bloom water. Keep dogs and kids away from shoreline scums and avoid recreation in visibly affected areas.

3

🌱 Be part of the fix

Use fertilizer wisely based on soil tests, plant shoreline buffers, pick up pet waste, and reduce lawn runoff.

4

📱 Stay informed

Check OK Travel lake conditions before every visit. Report suspected blooms to ODEQ.
